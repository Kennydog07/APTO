AI Productivity Issue 1

The ChatGPT prompt that writes your emails in 90 seconds

Tuesday, 3 June 2026 · 2 min read · Free

Hey,

Quick one this week — because this prompt is too good not to share immediately.

I've been running a small business for a while now, and the task that ate the most time every single week wasn't strategy. Wasn't the actual work. It was email.

Follow-ups. Proposals. Checking in after meetings. Cold outreach. Responding to complaints. Every one of them started with a blank page and ended with me rewriting the same sentence four times.

Then I started using this prompt.

The prompt

Copy this → paste into ChatGPT free
"You are a professional email writer for a small business owner. Write a follow-up email to [client name] about [the proposal / project / meeting / invoice]. Keep it under 80 words. Tone: friendly but direct. End with one clear next step. No filler phrases like 'I hope this finds you well.'"

Replace the brackets with your specifics. Paste into ChatGPT — the free version works perfectly. Hit enter.

What comes back is a clean, professional first draft in about 90 seconds. Always edit AI output before you send it — but you're editing, not starting from nothing. That's the difference.

Why this prompt actually works

Most AI email prompts fail because they're too vague. "Write me a follow-up email" gives you something generic. This prompt gives ChatGPT four specific constraints: length, tone, structure and what to leave out. The more specific your instructions, the better the output.

The rule: Treat ChatGPT like a capable new hire who doesn't know your business yet. You wouldn't say "write me an email." You'd say "write a follow-up to Sarah at Acme about the proposal I sent Monday — keep it short, friendly, and ask if she has questions before Friday."

Adapt it for anything

The same structure works for any email situation:

  • Cold outreach: "Write a cold outreach email to [type of business] introducing [your service]. Lead with one specific insight. Under 100 words. No generic openers."
  • Complaint response: "Write a polite response to a customer who complained about [issue]. Acknowledge, apologise briefly, explain the next step. Under 120 words."
  • Re-engagement: "Write an email to [client] who went quiet two weeks ago. Friendly, not pushy. Add one small piece of value. Leave the door open. Under 80 words."
  • Proposal follow-up: "Write a follow-up to [client] about a proposal sent on [date]. Short. Assume they're busy not uninterested. One clear next step."

Save the ones that work. You'll use them every week.

This week's action
Pick one email you've been putting off this week. Use the prompt above. Send it. That's it.
